Week 3: Systematic Literature Review

Understanding the procedure and processes in conducting a Systematic Literature Review

Week Overview

This week introduces the systematic literature review process and explains how to conduct a rigorous and structured research synthesis. Emphasis is placed on formulating clear research questions, developing a review protocol, conducting comprehensive database searches, screening studies based on eligibility criteria, assessing study quality, extracting and synthesizing data using tables or matrices, and effectively presenting findings. The focus is on ensuring transparency, replicability, and methodological rigor throughout the review process.

Learning Objectives:

  • LO1: Define Research Question that the review aims.
  • LO2: Develop a Review Protocol.
  • LO3: Conduct a comprehensive search of multiple database.
  • LO4: Screen studies for eligibility.
  • LO5: Assess the quality of the studies included in the research.
  • LO6: Extract Data and synthesize using tables or matrices.
  • LO7: Present your findings.

Research Question

A clear question guides the scope and direction of the entire review process.

1. Determining Scope and Relevance

The research question sets the boundaries for what is included in the review. It ensures that the search for literature is directed and relevant, addressing the specific aspects or dimensions of the topic in question.

2. Criteria for Inclusion and Synthesis

The research question also aids in determining the inclusion criteria for studies and guides the synthesis of the findings. It ensures that the collected data directly address the question, making the synthesis both relevant and focused.

Formulating Research Questions for ICT and SDGs

Introduction to Research Question Frameworks

Structured research questions play a crucial role in shaping a Systematic Literature Review (SLR). They guide the literature search, define scope boundaries, and influence interpretation of findings within ICT and Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). Effective research questions ensure clarity, relevance, and methodological rigor.

Overview of Frameworks

Different research frameworks are selected depending on the nature of the inquiry. Quantitative intervention-based studies often use structured models like PICO, while qualitative and exploratory studies may benefit from flexible frameworks such as SPIDER or PICo.

PICO(Quantitative Research)

Population, Intervention, Comparison, Outcome

Best suited for quantitative intervention-based research.


Example Topic: AI-Based Tutoring System
  • Population: Undergraduate computer science students in state universities
  • Intervention: AI-based adaptive tutoring system
  • Comparison: Traditional lecture-based instruction
  • Outcome: Improved academic performance and retention rates
"Among undergraduate computer science students, does the use of an AI-based adaptive tutoring system improve academic performance compared to traditional lecture-based instruction?

PICo(Qualitative Research)

Population, Interest, Context


Example Topic: Faculty Experience Using AI Tools
  • Population: University faculty members
  • Interest: Experiences and perceptions of using AI tools in teaching
  • Context: Higher education institutions implementing blended learning
"How do university faculty members experience and perceive the integration of AI tools in blended learning environments?"

SPIDER(Mixed-Methods)

Sample, Phenomenon of Interest, Design, Evaluation, Research Type

Ideal for mixed method ICT studies.


Example Topic: IoT-Based Fire Monitoring System
  • Sample: Local disaster risk reduction officers
  • Phenomenon of Interest: Use of IoT-based fire monitoring systems
  • Design: Semi-structured interviews
  • Evaluation: Perceived effectiveness and usability
  • Research Type: Mixed-Method
"How do disaster risk reduction officers evaluate the effectiveness and usability of IoT-based fire monitoring systems?

CIMO(Management or Applied Research)

Context, Intervention, Mechanism, Outcome


Example Topic: Digital Transformation in Agriculture
  • Context: Small rice farmers in Northern Mindanao
  • Intervention: Digital credit access platform
  • Mechanism:Improved transparency, faster loan approval, and reduced transaction costs
  • Outcome: Increased financial inclusion and farm productivity
"In the context of small rice farmers in Northern Mindanao, how does the implementation of a digital credit access platform improve financial inclusion through enhanced transparency and reduced transaction costs?"

Conclusion

These research frameworks help structure ICT-related inquiries aligned with Sustainable Development Goals. Selecting the appropriate framework ensures methodological alignment, clarity, and relevance in systematic reviews and empirical studies.

Understanding Different Frameworks

Several structured frameworks are available for formulating research questions in ICT and SDG-related studies. These include PICO, SPIDER, PICo and CIMO. Each framework possesses unique characteristics that make it suitable for different research designs and inquiry types.

Assessing the Strengths and Limitations

Framework Best For Strengths Limitations
PICO Quantitative intervention-based studies
  • Provides clear and measurable components
  • Highly structured and precise
  • May be too rigid for exploratory or qualitative research
SPIDER Qualitative and mixed-methods research
  • Flexible for exploring broader phenomena
  • Captures experiences and perceptions effectively
  • Less structured for strict intervention evaluation
PICo Qualitative explorations
  • Broad and adaptable scope
  • Useful for contextual and experience-based studies
  • May lack specificity for quantitative impact measurement
CIMO Organizational and management studies
  • Strong emphasis on context and mechanisms
  • Suitable for ICT implementation and policy research

Criteria for Choosing a Framework

Research Aims

  • If evaluating a specific ICT intervention → PICO
  • If exploring experiences or perceptions → SPIDER / PICo
  • If analyzing context or mechanisms → CIMO

Nature of the ICT Project

  • Technology Development → Structured models like PICO
  • Implementation Studies → CIMO

Alignment with SDGs

  • Health impact measurement (SDG 3) → PICO
  • Education transformation (SDG 4) → PICo / SPIDER

Practical Application

When applying a chosen framework, clearly define each component based on the ICT project's scope and SDG alignment. For example, in PICO, precisely define the target population, the ICT intervention, the comparison condition, and the measurable outcomes linked to the specific Sustainable Development Goal.

Consideration of Research Methodology

The selected framework must align with the overall research methodology:

  • Qualitative methodologies → SPIDER, PICo
  • Quantitative methodologies → PICO
  • Mixed-methods approaches → SPIDER or CIMO

Ensuring methodological alignment strengthens the rigor, coherence, and validity of the research design, particularly in ICT projects aimed at addressing Sustainable Development Goals.

Review Protocol

It is a Systematic Procedures/ Techniques & Tools to carry out the research.

Importance of a Structured Protocol in Systematic Literature Reviews (SLRs)

  • Systematic Foundation: A structured review protocol serves as the backbone of a successful Systematic Literature Review (SLR). It provides a step-by-step plan guiding the entire review process, ensuring consistency, completeness, and methodological rigor while preventing the omission of critical studies.
  • Minimizing Bias: Predetermined criteria and procedures reduce subjective bias. Treating all studies uniformly ensures objectivity in selection, evaluation, and interpretation of evidence.
  • Replicability & Transparency: A clearly defined protocol enhances replicability. Other researchers can follow the same process and achieve comparable findings—an essential principle in ICT and sustainability research.
  • Comprehensive Data Collection: The protocol defines databases, search strategies, keywords, and synthesis methods, ensuring thorough and methodologically sound data collection and analysis.
  • Credibility & Validity: Following a structured protocol strengthens the credibility of findings by demonstrating systematic and rigorous adherence to recognized research standards.
  • Clarity & Direction: A protocol provides focus, manages scope, and aligns the research team with study objectives, particularly in complex ICT-SDG research areas.

Components of a Review Protocol for Systematic Literature Reviews

1. Keywords & Boolean Operators

How to Identify Important Keywords, Terms, or Synonyms

To develop a protocol, you need to collect a list of terms that you will use to retrieve relevant studies during the search process. Remember that authors may use different words to describe a search concept, so you need to collate a list of alternative words and synonyms. If you fail to include all the relevant terms in the search, you may miss out on appropriate studies.

Methods and Tools to Identify Keywords and Synonyms
  1. Brainstorming: List potential alternative terms or synonyms for the search concepts. Think about different words, phrases, and jargon that may be used in the field. Perform a simple preliminary search on your main concepts – titles, abstracts, and keywords of alternative terms.
  2. Identify Subject-Specific Terms: Use textbooks, lecture notes, encyclopedias, and dictionaries to find relevant terms.
  3. Thesaurus and Controlled Vocabulary: Consult these to find standardized terms and synonyms.
  4. Review References: Look at reference lists and other systematic reviews to identify additional terms and phrases that have been used to describe the search concepts.
  5. Use Search Engines: Identify related terms and phrases that appear frequently in the literature.
  6. Consult a Librarian: Librarians familiar with your study can provide guidance on developing comprehensive search strategies.
  7. Use Tools:
    • WordFreq – to analyze term frequency
    • Citation Chaser – to track related citations

Develop comprehensive keywords and use Boolean operators (AND, OR, NOT).

Example: "IoT AND healthcare NOT industrial"

Define a time frame (e.g., last 5–10 years) to ensure relevance.

Example: Sensor-Based Systems in Agriculture

Context of the Example

This Systematic Literature Review (SLR) explores sensor-based systems in agriculture, an area where advanced ICT technologies intersect with sustainable development principles. The review focuses on how integrating sensor technologies into agricultural practices optimizes crop yield and improves resource management efficiency.

Relevance to Agriculture and Sustainable Development

Alignment with SDGs

  • SDG 2: Zero Hunger
  • SDG 12: Responsible Consumption and Production

Sensor technologies enhance productivity, minimize waste, and optimize water and soil nutrient use.

Technological Intersection

  • Soil moisture sensors
  • Climate condition monitors
  • Drones
  • Satellite imagery

The SLR examines implementation contexts and measurable agricultural outcomes.

Research Significance

Aim of the SLR

As global population growth increases resource pressure, innovative agricultural technologies become essential. This review analyzes the development, implementation, effectiveness, and limitations of sensor-based systems.

Contribution to Knowledge and Practice

The review synthesizes findings to inform farmers, agri-businesses, researchers, and policy-makers about benefits, challenges, and socio-economic implications of sensor adoption.

Applying the Protocol Components

1. Literature Search Strategy

Defining Keywords
“Agricultural sensors”, “Precision farming”, “Sensor technology in agriculture”, “Sustainable agriculture technology”
Combining Keywords
“Agricultural sensors” AND “crop yield”
“Precision farming” OR “sustainable agriculture technology”
Selecting Databases
  • Scopus
  • IEEE Xplore
  • Web of Science
  • AGRIS
  • CAB Abstracts

2. Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria

Inclusion Criteria
  • Peer-reviewed articles (last 10 years)
  • Field-based sensor technologies
  • Direct outcomes (yield improvement, efficiency)
Exclusion Criteria
  • Non-peer-reviewed articles
  • Studies older than 10 years
  • Non-English (if limited by language)
  • Indoor farming or unrelated technologies

3. Data Extraction

Data Extraction Template Fields
  • Bibliographic details (title, authors, year)
  • Type of sensors and applications
  • Implementation strategy and geographic context
  • Challenges in deployment
  • Measured outcomes (yield, efficiency, environmental impact)

Pilot Testing: Test the template on selected studies before full extraction.

4. Data Synthesis

Quantitative Synthesis

Analyze statistical improvements in crop yield and resource efficiency to identify trends.

Qualitative Synthesis

Conduct thematic analysis to extract patterns related to implementation challenges, farmer experiences, and technology effectiveness.

Integrated Analysis

Combine quantitative and qualitative findings to provide a comprehensive understanding of impact and sustainability outcomes.

Conclusion

By systematically applying structured protocol components, this SLR ensures a balanced, rigorous, and comprehensive evaluation of sensor-based systems in agriculture. The review bridges advanced ICT technologies with sustainable agricultural practices, highlighting technical innovation alongside socio-economic and environmental implications.

Search Process After Collating Key Terms

Once you have collated your key terms and considered the search limits, your search process begins:

  1. Line by Line: Each search term on its own line.

    Example for IoT/Embedded/AI:

                                "Internet of Things"
                            "Embedded Systems"
                            "Artificial Intelligence"
  2. Block by Block: Each search concept on its own line.

    Example for IoT/Embedded/AI:

                                ("Internet of Things" OR IoT)
                            AND
                            ("Embedded Systems" OR Embedded)
                            AND
                            ("Artificial Intelligence" OR AI)
  3. Single Line: All search terms and concepts combined into one line.

    Example for IoT/Embedded/AI:

                                ("Internet of Things" OR IoT) AND ("Embedded Systems" OR Embedded) AND ("Artificial Intelligence" OR AI)

Reviewing Your Search Results

After the search, review your results and consider the following:

  1. Can these results answer the review questions?
  2. Are the results relevant?
  3. Are too many or too few results being retrieved?
  4. Are the results retrieving key articles?

How to Document the Search

For each database search you conduct, you should record the following:

  1. The date the search was run
  2. The database searched
  3. The name of the platform used to search the database
  4. Your complete search strategy
  5. Any limits used (e.g., date, language, study design)

What is Grey Literature?

Grey literature refers to research and information produced outside of traditional academic or commercial publishing channels, such as government reports, working papers, theses, and conference proceedings. It is often unpublished or informally published, making it harder to locate. However, it provides current, in-depth, and specialized data that may not be available in peer-reviewed journals.

Assessing Grey Literature

  1. Source and Authorship
  2. Publication Type
  3. Methodology
  4. Results
  5. Relevance

Full-Text Review for Final Selection

  1. Retrieve full-text articles of all potentially eligible studies.
  2. Use a standardized review checklist to ensure consistency.
  3. Apply inclusion and exclusion criteria consistently across all studies.
  4. Conduct a quality assessment of each selected article.
  5. Document clear reasons for inclusion or exclusion.

Documentation and Record Keeping

  1. Search Documentation – Record databases searched, search terms used, dates of searches, and applied filters.
  2. Screening Logs – Maintain detailed records of title and abstract screening decisions.
  3. PRISMA Flow Diagram – Use a visual flow diagram to clearly present the study selection process.
  4. Secure Storage – Maintain digital backups and secure storage of all research records.

Building a Reference Library

  1. Select Reference Management Tools – Use tools such as EndNote, Zotero, Mendeley, or RefWorks to manage citations efficiently.
  2. Organize References – Use folders, tags, and keyword systems to categorize studies.
  3. Annotate Articles – Add notes and highlights to assist with data extraction and synthesis.
  4. Automate Citations – Generate in-text citations and bibliographies automatically using reference software.

Conclusion

A rigorous and well-planned literature search and selection process is foundational to conducting a robust and reliable Systematic Literature Review. Through structured searching, screening, documentation, and reference management, researchers ensure transparency, replicability, and methodological rigor in ICT and Sustainable Development research.
